The Box

Design Thinking and Communication Course II

Northwestern University

Time Frame March - June 2022
Problem With aging and lack of exercise, many older adults become dependent on caregivers or nursing homes. Without guidance and sufficient motivation, they may avoid exercise, which can gradually reduce their independence.
Solution A simple tool providing stable grounding for safe at-home workouts. A QR code offers short instructional videos, showing easy exercises to promote self-reliance and better health.

Background Research

Our Users

Our primary end users are the aging population or, as described by our project partners, “baby boomers” (people born in 1946-1964).

Secondary Research

We watched various videos and read articles about the physical exercises recommended for middle-aged people. Those exercises include light weight-lifting, running, yoga, cardio, and more.

Existing Solutions

We explored several existing at-home exercise tools to better understand current options available to older adults. It quickly became clear that many of these solutions aren't tailored specifically to the unique needs and limitations of our target demographic.

User Interview Insights

We conducted interviews with two older adults to better understand their specific needs and motivations.

Ms. Cindy Stewart (age 68) highlighted the value of personal interest in maintaining motivation: “Exercising feels much easier when it includes activities I genuinely enjoy. Maybe a reward system would also help keep me going.” She prefers workouts that are engaging without becoming overly demanding.

Ms. Eleanor (age 72) discussed practical factors like affordability and versatility: "I'd love something versatile for different exercises—but it needs to fit my budget." She currently performs simple stretches at her kitchen counter and uses specialized equipment for balance during physical therapy.

The Design


The Box is a fitness design that motivates and educates users 65 years and older who are impacted by lack of exercise and aging. The wooden box is supported with two hand railings to aid the user in doing tricky and more hazardous movements.



The box also has rubber on the edges to add friction so the box does not slip and cause damage to the user.


Finally, there is a QR code on the side of the box that a user will scan once they have the box. This will take them to a set of videos where they can pick from a multitude of different types of exercises.


Testing


User Survey Results


We conducted a survey with 23 participants aged 65 or above to gather feedback on our design. Participants overwhelmingly preferred instructional videos as the most effective medium for learning new exercises, with a clear preference for accessing these through QR codes. Most respondents found our design intuitive and effective for exercising, though some suggested additional features for improved motivation and usability.

User Testing & Results

Finally, we conducted user testing sessions to gather feedback on our prototype, capturing initial impressions and identifying areas for improvement. Generally, there were positive reactions toward the aesthetics, ergonomic benefits, and integrated video instructions of our prototype. However, users expressed safety concerns about rotating handles, handle stability, and the box's tendency to slip during use.

Iteration

Through user and self-testing, We discovered that applying too much pressure caused the bars to rotate, potentially posing safety risks. To address this issue, we added rectangular wooden supports on each side of the bars, effectively stabilizing them and preventing unintended movement.
